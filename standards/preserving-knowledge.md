### Navigability

To reach a destination, there needs to be intent, then action. Quick inquiry: why does education...

* Quiz students on specific maps, before giving them a GPS?
* Start young kids with individual letters of the alphabet, before words?
* Show students add, subtract, multiply, divide - before finally permitting a calculator in higher-ed?
* Show programmers the hard way of writing a solution, before the easy way?

I'll be honest, while school itself has been a lifetime ago now - it's done something for me that I think a lot of people overlook: understanding direction.
The learning material starts basic, and scales up, because when we finally reach those checkpoints of autonomy, by then our minds have solidified the "why", beyond the how. Seeing *why something works*, fundamentally shapes "Ok, this is the destination - x, y, and z make it possible in a, b, and c ways"

One of the most powerful things in a human's tool-belt, is the fact we can be really good at getting through ambiguity when we figure out how. But that always changes. (Especially now) . When we're tapped into the frameworks of why, it directs the how

Here are some other bite-sized applications of when there *isn't* direction

* What happens when you randomly (or guess) press buttons on a calculator? Does it magically give the square root of 2?

Interestingly in all these examples there's some key attributes:

- more direction seems to yield stronger results
- which means the picture of intent becomes more clear
- which means the next set of intents become clear when the previous result feeds the next action
- which ultimately scales up to something fundamentally sharper than all previous iterations

Simply put: you, getting stronger, means better results. Less mistakes and accuracy also saves money, and time, which means "Hey, this person / place / thing, is a great investment"

## What exactly should be preserved?

All of that granular explanation... but *what*?

Great question. And this touches on two different dynamics, stuff we keep automated, and others that you're heavily focused on.

### Stuff we keep automated

From conversations managers have discussed with me, it's interesting to hear this first dynamic. There's the idea that: a point exists where there's no use in controlling every single aspect of the operation.

To that I certainly understand. On one side, there were games written in full-on assembly. Should we write a AAA game today with assembly with equivalent unreal engine graphics, performance would be remarkable, amazing, and fast. But let's be honest, it would take a whole lot longer... in the scale of years or even decades to build a game like that. Not to mention the absolute pain it would be for everyone involved. There's more of a benefit for at least *some* automation here, more specifically a compiler for a programming language. Maybe even a tool such as an IDE for building 3D structures.

---

You scale up, those concepts get hardened, and with enough understanding we can abstract to something more broad and powerful based on intent & action.

### Others that you're heavily focused on

And yet that's the part that you have to really analyze.

1. What in this moment *can* be abstracted?
2. But what will cause hiccups where they aren't perfect?

And, that's where you come in. At least in my practice, while we *can* code with an LLM, sometimes it's accurate, other times it makes mistakes. While it *can* understand an API, it may attempt to guess output. And when it's time to abstract out? The components get harder to understand, therefore more (or less) context, therefore more things that may go wrong, if not created correctly.

In this moment, things can and will go wrong as things are being built and hardened. Since at this time (and it may change), the very components that LLMs need to code can be misunderstood, even when building it's own functions, there's still requirement for conductor, you, to help navigate through complex problems. When you understand that, it's possible to manipulate the "slot machine" into a stronger outcome.

### Learn by LLM

It gets interesting here folks. Before I hit this one, I want to fully acknowledge that before doing this, *first* it's ideal to be talking with your local humans in addition to this action. The very humans alongside you can very much assist in finding hindsights that a language model can't. Aspects like language quirks that didn't make it into model training, practicality, standards, and business context that just doesn't make it into the everyday prompt or LLM PRD. All that is crucial

Ok, *now* let's dive into this, because it's worth handling with care. If done right though, the gains have excellent returns.

---

Large language models are contextual machines. It's strength is synthesizing the understanding of information, by aligning on how someone speaks with nuance, speech, and previous understanding. It can mirror back things in ways you had trouble understanding because of the way our minds can pinhole specific concepts.

That's something internet forums or blog posts may struggle with if they don't speak in the fashion that makes sense for someone else. (And yes, this does mean that this blog post might even benefit to be synthesized differently, if the way I write this doesn't click). LLMs are very good at this, by re-chunking the info into bits that make sense.

Alright, so we're looking to learn:

* what can be done
* what a plan entails
* or even what's already done

The simplest form of doing any of the three is as just by asking.

### What can be done / What a plan entails

> I'm seeking to build a material UI project; though what options are possible? I see that material UI table is a solid option, but what options could also be taken on? I see that material UI already does have something, but is that sufficient over the former? Let's figure out the pros & cons of the possibilities

The above goes beyond a simple prompt. Not only does it talk about the intent, it's personalized with any questions you might be facing about a best-applied solution. It's also introducing thoughtful objectivity, by figuring out what might be good or bad, without simply a blind trust behind a potential pick from the LLM.

The model might discuss one solution, but you might see that an alternative solution would fair *much* better. But that ultimately depends on what you find.

> Ok, I see that you picked XYZ... interesting - can you tell me more? I'm not familiar with this framework, so are there external links to learning more?

This will outline both a general introduction based on your own lingo, and a place to see how the fundamentals work. An agentic LLM can search the web and get a resource to move forward in the right place. (subjectively, that's where checking the actual resource it may or may not pick is relevant)

> I see, I know XYZ better now, but ABC may also pose benefits from what I'm reading - thoughts? What are pros & cons of both?

Presuming you learned about both, seeing another set of eyes will have the model consider things. It's even possible, that based on either new or existing info, the model might even change its mind. But just because *the model does*, doesn't mean you have to. This is part of the mindful process of building something concrete.

This is high-level planning, but not fully delegated. The reason for contextual information in the first place is to strike a middle ground between understandings. When you're confidently one with the plan (don't cheat!) Let's move on.

### What's already done

Things are built or perhaps the framework of the application is already present. It was constructed by many before you so the context is large. Let's drill:

> Can you explain what this component is doing in the file?

An explanation is provided and it's large. However the overview helps to better understand why the component is coded the way it is. Though there are pending questions:

> If that's the case, why does XYZ component behave in this way?

The model dives deeper. You may also be in the process of using a debugger or the react component viewer on the web-browser. The understanding becomes more nuanced, but also you notice a quirk while debugging:

> It appears <x detail> is causing the component to stray away from it's behavior

You've successfully given the LLM more contextual information for why something is that way! It's angle is adjusted, and now it may point something out that it didn't notice before. Is that correct though? Well it's then possible to view the code and verify it's working / may work the way the model is attempting to convey. If it's true, then your understanding improves, and therefore the model can be better steered. The model after all may also do it's own pinhole-ing and very much focus on your intents, causing the action to be heavily skewed.

By keeping your brain on while the model is also taking action, this feedback loop of doing, learning, adjusting, allows things to grow with good accuracy.

Again this is one tool in entire belt of using docs, debugging, and solving the issue in the end.

## LLM: Quiz me!

You've drilled into the context, and solved a big problem today with the power of contextual understanding. That's perhaps one method, but there's another that can drill it down *even more*.

The LLM may know the context behind what might be learned, big or small... but do you? The language model has a capacity to share this information in a way that forces you to think. IDEs also come with a "question tool", to allow for pre-defined responses. The combination? A trivia game specifically tailored to you

An example prompt:

> Use the "question" tool to ask me 3 questions about a topic. If answered correctly, prompt again for additional questions. If answered incorrectly, ask the user if they'd like tailored questions based on what they should learn.

This is a feedback loop that continues to iterate upon itself. Guess correctly, you've learned. Otherwise, it's a way to get more information out of something you may have missed. Ultimately, forcing you to think and even viewing the source material and getting deeper.

There's also nothing inherently wrong with asking for a deeper explanation for why something is. This can then get you prepped for further questions potentially being asked.

The more this is done, the greater the understanding of something you may be struggling with, because it's interactive, not passive.

---

One obvious glare though is important to take note of: if the LLM gets the facts the wrong, the trivia will also be wrong, and therefore you learn the wrong thing. Therefore it's important that:

* You yourself understand source material it's getting from
* The source material the model is getting from is reputable
* If the source material is the code itself, ensure that looks good before finally quizzing

Another protip - you could also ask the model to view specific source material you wanted to study, *then* asking *you* to navigate to specific parts of the docs it searched through. You're effectively putting pen & paper down this way, each time you make an impression like that, the brain is being put to work, which builds that muscle necessary to be awake and get that necessary understanding.
